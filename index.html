UX | Improving the Usability of Existing Analytic Dashboards

Overview

This initiative centered around improving a web appâ€™s existing analytic dashboard framework by addressing post-MVP painpoints in dashboard management, ease of use, and speed of creation.

    Challenges

  • Accomplish project over time with tiered stages of improvements.
  • Solve for multiple usability challenges at each step.
  • Careful planning around introduction of features for live customers.

    Goals

  • Create a streamlined update to the dashboard framework.
  • Increase the number of dashboards created by users.
  • Increase confidence in editing of dashboards in users.
  • Make the feature set friendly to a wide variety of personas.

    Good to Know

  • The purpose of the dashboards for users were to create an overview of marketing and communication presence and effect in their industry.
  • The existing framework was brought to market in a fast, MVP fashion and was now ready to be iterated on after early adoption.
  • The framework would be used in multiple places throughout the app.
  • Desktop users were the prescribed target audience.

    Role

  • I was involved in multiple fashions throughout this project, including wireframing, user flow, UI, discovery research, and usability testing.

Design Process

Before

Discovery

Through stakeholder feedback we generated some areas to begin exploring later with research and design. Several stakeholders had the thought that users would be more likely to adopt dashboards if they had more control over them, and could customizethem with ease. Most stakeholders believed that dashboards were formidable and that there was much to be desired in terms of on-boarding.

Research

Through user research we came away with a few key insights, and validated and invalidated some early assumptions:

  • Users found dashboard creation to be tedious.
  • Some users did not know that they were editable at all.
  • A large number of steps were required to edit charts and groupings.
  • UI was not streamlined and outdated.
  • Users were unfamiliar with many of the possible data types.
  • Users appeared to use their dashboards in a wide variety of ways.
  • Usersâ€™ first dashboards were created by support specialists.
  • Users generally used their dashboards for periodic reporting but would use their dashboards to generate assets for other reporting channels.

Design

We began by tackling the flow and UI of the dashboard system in general. I focused on rearranging charts, grouping charts together, resizing charts, as well as an overall updated look.

Various scenarios and variations were created to account for various state and edge cases, with prototypes to test with and share specifications and scenarios with Developers.

The second phase of the project was to improve the editing of a chart. The research showed users spent wasted amounts of time creating new charts because editing older charts wasz not flexible. I tried to expand the options within the editing modal to help the users with their reporting needs.

The third phase of the project was to improve the speed of creating a dashboard. However, there were many ways we could go about this, so I sought to understand how users were grouping and creating their dashboards currently by examining server-side data with the help of some analysts.

I learned that A) users created sections within existing dashboards over creating new dashboards, B) they seemed to be treating their dashboards with scoped context â€“ meaning they would make one for a period of time, or for a particular campaign/theme. This allowed me to make the recommendation to product owners that we focus mostly on allowing them to add pre-assembled bundles of charts rather than pre-assembled dashboards, as users would be looking to build on existing dashboards.

I then began architecting solutions for creating bundles of charts for other designers who would focus on the UI later.

Then I wireframed the start of a clickable prototype.

Iteration

Over time this project was completed in an ongoing, agile fashion. Throughout the process, we utilized feedback sessions with users and internal stakeholders to iterate and learn from our designs. Testing showed a positive reaction to the new framework and validated our ideas for dashboard duplication, and avoiding modals. However, we did iterate over time due to negative feedback on section editing, and the placement of controls for charts.

Results and Conclusions

Results

Long term behavioral feedback showed that users were not utilizing Section controls in the new framework, and less effort was put forth toward those features.

Usage of dashboards showed a steady increase, and usage of section bundles was higher than blank sections beforehand.

Qualitative feedback was positive for the features that made it to production.

Next Steps

Continue to improve on reporting features for users, as this was their primary end-goal, such as the ability to export different types of reports.

Eventually I would go on to work on improving the reports from the app as a next step.

Learning Opportunities

This project was a little more waterfall than I would have liked, but at times we were strapped for users to test with as the demographic on it was small (at the time).

Utilizing data before making product decisions is always a positive, and allowed us to invest in the right calories for section creation.
